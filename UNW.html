Agenda Item I

Selective Solidarity: Western Feminist Complicity, Reproductive Violence in Gaza, the Decolonisation of the Women's Rights Movement, and the Failure to Implement UN Security Council Resolution 1325

Introduction

The global women's rights movement has historically advocated for gender equality, reproductive rights, and protection from violence. However, recent conflicts, particularly in Gaza, have sparked debates regarding the consistency of international feminist advocacy. Critics argue that some Western feminist organizations have been selective in their responses to violence against women, especially in conflict zones involving geopolitical interests. This agenda examines allegations of reproductive violence in Gaza, the implementation failures of UN Security Council Resolution 1325, and broader calls to decolonize international feminist movements.

History and Background

  • In 2000, the United Nations adopted UN Security Council Resolution 1325 (UNSCR 1325) on Women, Peace, and Security.
  • The resolution recognized the disproportionate impact of armed conflict on women and called for their inclusion in peacebuilding and conflict resolution.
  • Numerous conflicts worldwide have demonstrated that women and girls often face gender-based violence, displacement, and restricted access to healthcare.
  • The Palestinian-Israeli conflict has generated ongoing concerns regarding women's rights, reproductive healthcare, and civilian protection.
  • Critics argue that international feminist movements have not always responded consistently to violations affecting women in non-Western regions.
  • The concept of decolonizing feminism emerged from concerns that global feminist discourse often prioritizes Western perspectives while marginalizing voices from the Global South.

Main Problems

1. Inadequate Implementation of UNSCR 1325

Many states have failed to fully implement commitments regarding women's participation in peace and security processes.

2. Reproductive Violence in Conflict Zones

Women in conflict areas often face restricted access to maternal healthcare, reproductive services, and essential medical supplies.

3. Selective Advocacy

Perceived inconsistency in international responses to women's suffering undermines the credibility of global human rights movements.

4. Exclusion of Local Voices

Women from conflict-affected regions are often underrepresented in international policymaking and advocacy platforms.

5. Gender-Based Violence During Armed Conflict

Conflict environments increase risks of sexual violence, exploitation, and abuse.

6. Humanitarian Access Restrictions

Limited access to healthcare, food, water, and shelter disproportionately affects women and children.

Council Stakeholders

UN Security Council

Responsible for overseeing the Women, Peace, and Security agenda and promoting UNSCR 1325 implementation.

Women in Conflict Zones

Directly affected by armed conflict, displacement, and acute humanitarian crises.

Governments in Conflict

Responsible for ensuring compliance with international humanitarian and human rights law.

Feminist Organizations

Advocate for gender equality and women's rights globally, though subject to criticism regarding selective advocacy.

Humanitarian Orgs

Provide critical frontline healthcare, protection mechanisms, and emergency assistance.

Civil Society Groups

Represent local communities and advocate directly for affected populations on the ground.

International Community

Provides essential diplomatic frameworks, financial resources, and humanitarian support networks.

Multidimensional Impacts

Humanitarian

  • Increased maternal health risks.
  • Limited access to reproductive healthcare.
  • Higher levels of displacement and vulnerability.

Social

  • Long-term trauma among women and families.
  • Disruption of education and community structures.

Political

  • Reduced trust in international institutions.
  • Growing debates regarding representation within global feminist movements.

Security

  • Exclusion of women from peace processes may reduce the effectiveness of conflict resolution efforts.

Feasible Solutions Framework

01. Strengthen UNSCR 1325

Require states to submit regular, rigorous reports on their Women, Peace, and Security commitments.

02. Ensure Humanitarian Access

Facilitate safe and uninterrupted delivery of essential healthcare, reproductive services, and humanitarian assistance.

03. Increase Women's Participation

Promote meaningful, direct participation of women in active peace negotiations and post-conflict reconstruction.

04. Support Local Organizations

Provide direct funding, operational resources, and institutional support to grassroots women's organizations.

05. Independent Monitoring

Establish mechanisms to independently monitor compliance with international humanitarian and human rights obligations.

06. Inclusive Feminist Dialogue

Encourage greater, formalized representation of women from conflict-affected and developing regions in international forums.

Agenda Item II

Wonderwomen or Warning Sign: Redrafting CEDAW and the CRC

Introduction

The Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) and the Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) are among the most influential international human rights treaties. While they have significantly advanced protections for women and children, changing social, technological, economic, and geopolitical realities have led to calls for reform. This agenda examines whether aspects of these treaties require updating to address contemporary challenges while preserving their core principles.

History and Background

CEDAW

  • Adopted by the United Nations General Assembly in 1979.
  • Often referred to as the "international bill of rights for women."
  • Seeks to eliminate discrimination against women in political, economic, social, and cultural spheres.

CRC

  • Adopted in 1989.
  • Establishes comprehensive rights for children, including protection, development, participation, and survival rights.
  • One of the most widely ratified international treaties.

Emerging Modern Challenges

  • Digital technologies and online exploitation.
  • Artificial intelligence and data privacy concerns.
  • New forms of gender discrimination.
  • Climate change and displacement.
  • Evolving family structures and social norms.

These developments have prompted discussions regarding whether existing treaty frameworks adequately address modern realities.

Main Problems

1. Outdated Provisions

Certain treaty provisions were drafted before the emergence of modern technological and social challenges.

2. Inconsistent Implementation

Many countries have ratified the treaties but have not fully implemented their obligations.

3. Digital Threats

Children and women face cyberbullying, online exploitation, trafficking, and privacy violations.

4. Enforcement Gaps

International treaties often lack strong, actionable enforcement mechanisms.

5. Cultural and Political Disagreements

States may fundamentally disagree on contemporary interpretations of rights and obligations.

6. Emerging Humanitarian Challenges

Climate-induced displacement and prolonged conflicts create totally new vulnerabilities for women and children.

Intersecting Stakeholders

United Nations

Responsible for monitoring treaty implementation and facilitating necessary institutional reforms.

Member States

Sovereign signatories legally responsible for executing and implementing treaty obligations domestically.

Women & Girls

The primary core beneficiaries of the protections laid out under CEDAW.

Children & Youth

The primary demographic beneficiaries of the protections instituted under the CRC.

Human Rights Orgs

Advocate fiercely for stronger treaty interpretations, robust protections, and strict implementation.

Educational Institutions

Play an essential role in promoting grassroots awareness, civic literacy, and protecting rights.

Technology Companies

Increasingly powerful non-state actors deeply influential regarding online safety, algorithm bias, data privacy, and modern digital rights.

Intersectional Impacts

Social

  • Greater protection of vulnerable populations.
  • Improved access to education and healthcare.

Political

  • Enhanced international cooperation on human rights.
  • Potential disagreements over treaty reform semantics.

Economic

  • Improved participation of women and children in economic development.
  • Increased investment in social welfare systems.

Technological

  • Better safeguards against online abuse and exploitation.
  • Stronger, codified digital rights protections.

Reform & Protocol Solutions

01. Draft Optional Protocols

Update protections through additional, agile protocols addressing modern challenges rather than fully replacing legacy treaties.

02. Strengthen Monitoring

Improve state reporting requirements, compliance reviews, and data transparency mandates.

03. Address Digital Rights

Include modern provisions concerning cybersecurity, algorithmic bias, online exploitation, privacy, and AI-related risks.

04. Displaced Populations

Enhance specific legal protections for refugees, internally displaced persons (IDPs), and climate-induced migrants.

05. International Cooperation

Encourage rapid information sharing, mutual legal assistance, and best-practice exchanges among member states.

06. Education & Participation

Increase public awareness programs and ensure that affected groups (youth and women) participate directly in treaty reform discourse.

Key Committee Perspective

These topics are highly normative and politically sensitive. Delegates must carefully navigate diplomatic fault lines while advocating for universal protections. Focus heavily on:

  • International human rights law
  • Gender equality & women's participation
  • Protection of civilians during conflict
  • Treaty implementation & strategic reform
  • Humanitarian access & accountability
  • Multilateral cross-border cooperation

Resolution Mandate: Successful resolutions should balance robust human rights protections, state sovereignty, practical implementation mechanisms, and international cooperation while remaining strictly consistent with existing UN frameworks.
